# Abstract V2

Structural priors can guide few-shot code generation, but their evaluation is confounded when structural prompting, information access, candidate budget, and execution-based selection change together. We ask: after controlling test-time opportunity and disclosing information access, what structural-prior claim remains warranted? We introduce **SPC-Audit**, a fair-budget diagnostic audit for structural-prior claims in execution-evaluated Python function generation. It matches candidate budget and execution-call accounting, reports information access, separates diagnostic from deployable selection, adds boundary controls, and records explicit non-claims. On `MBPP+224`, an uncontrolled comparison suggests a large gain, `167/224 -> 184/224`; under the controlled diagnostic comparison, the warranted claim narrows to fixed code-aware `syntax_aware` episodes: `no_prior + MBR` solves `178.67 +/- 0.58`, while `multi_prior + MBR` solves `185.00 +/- 1.73`. Prompt-only controls bound the claim: the full `MBPP+224` prompt-structural rerun is non-positive. A retrospective prior-quality response audit is consistent with this boundary: intended code-aware priors are positive in medium/high diagnostic-fidelity bins, while the full prompt-only rerun rarely constructs high-fidelity priors. The central methodological lesson is that structural-prior evaluations should report quality coverage, quality-conditioned net delta, harm rate, claim survival, and explicit non-claims, not only an average prior-versus-no-prior delta. The contribution is a reusable E&D reporting pattern and claim-audited artifact, not a deployable structural-prior method, new benchmark, or strongest-method result.
